Journey for Justice: Ju Hong's Story

Struggles and Injustice

Ju Hong came from a lower-income background and his family struggled to deal with debts. He also dealt with troubles at home before moving to the United States. His mother decided to take the chance and move to a completely unfamiliar land which they knew almost nothing about, to find new opportunities for a better life. There was just nothing left in South Korea for them anymore. Ju initially struggled to assimilate into American life, but if that wasn’t enough, he soon had to deal with being undocumented - which he soon found out was more than just a label.[5]

The Harsh Reality

Ju Hong’s first acceptance letter was to U.C Davis, and he was accepted to other renowned universities and colleges. He was overjoyed and thought that his hard work had finally paid off. However, the reality soon hit him: he would be ineligible for financial aid due to his immigration status. It would have been incredibly expensive for him to enroll. Ju’s mother was already struggling to pay for their rent. He eventually had to turn most of his offers down and had to attend a community college instead. This is where he started to learn about what it meant to be undocumented.[5]

I'm Not Alone

Initially, Ju felt left out when he heard other students talking about which colleges they would be attending. He felt like a completely different person, stopped interacting with most of his friends, and started becoming depressed. It was a difficult period in life for him. However, he slowly began finding stories of other undocumented immigrants. Meeting up and having meaningful conversations with other students in exact or similar positions as Ju really shaped the trajectory of Ju’s life for the better. Ju realized the lack of public information and also realized that a lot more students were like him, which made him feel much better and inspired about his situation.[5]

Becoming an Advocate

Perseverance and Growth

The DREAM Act evidently meant a lot to Ju Hong, because it gave him the chance to not have to pay an enormous amount to college when it was unfeasible for his family, and it gave Ju the chance to continue speaking out at quite a few different avenues. Apart from his self-started blog and his in-school involvements, Ju’s impact grew as he additionally worked with “did phone banking, press conferences, petition drives, events and rallies,” and more too (Hong). Furthermore, additionally from Ju’s perspective, we can see the extensive portfolio of involvement he built up when he tells us, “I spoke at many different conferences, and I wrote my own opinion column for a Berkeley newspaper. I also did interviews with the national media and shared my testimony.” (Hong). Take it from Ju: every step is meaningful, even if in the moment, nothing seems to be meaningful. It will end up being fine![5]
